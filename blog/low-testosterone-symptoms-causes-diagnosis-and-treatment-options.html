Low Testosterone Symptoms: Causes, Diagnosis, and Treatment Options

Contents
March 9, 2026

Low testosterone is more common than most men realize, and it's easy to miss. The symptoms — fatigue, low sex drive, weight gain, brain fog — often get written off as stress or normal aging. But for many men, the root cause is a measurable hormonal deficiency that responds well to treatment.

Testosterone naturally declines about 1% per year after age 30. For some men, that decline is gradual and manageable. For others, levels drop enough to meaningfully affect quality of life. When that happens, identifying the cause and choosing the right treatment can make a significant difference.

This guide covers everything you need to know: the most common symptoms of low testosterone, what causes levels to fall, how it's diagnosed, and which treatment options are available.

What Is Low Testosterone?

Testosterone is the primary male sex hormone. It plays a central role in:

  • Sex drive and erectile function
  • Muscle mass and strength
  • Fat distribution and metabolism
  • Bone density
  • Mood, motivation, and cognitive function
  • Red blood cell production

Clinically, low testosterone — also called hypogonadism — is typically defined as a total testosterone level below 300 ng/dL, combined with symptoms. The number alone doesn't tell the full story; symptoms matter just as much as lab values when determining whether treatment is appropriate.

Common Symptoms of Low Testosterone

Low testosterone symptoms are often gradual and easy to overlook. Many men attribute them to aging, poor sleep, or work stress — sometimes for years before getting tested.

Sexual Symptoms

The most commonly reported early signs are sexual. Men with low testosterone often notice a reduced sex drive, fewer spontaneous erections, decreased semen volume, or difficulty achieving and maintaining erections. Testosterone plays a foundational role in libido and erectile function, so sexual symptoms are frequently the first signal that levels have dropped.

Physical Symptoms

Low testosterone can significantly alter body composition over time. Men often experience:

  • Loss of lean muscle mass even with regular exercise
  • Increased abdominal fat that resists diet and training
  • Reduced physical endurance
  • Persistent fatigue
  • Gradual thinning of body hair

These changes happen slowly, which is why many men don't connect them to a hormonal issue until they're well established.

Mental and Emotional Symptoms

Testosterone affects the brain as well as the body. Men with low T frequently report:

  • Brain fog and difficulty concentrating
  • Low motivation
  • Irritability
  • Reduced confidence
  • Mild depressive symptoms

Because these overlap with common mental health conditions, low testosterone is often underdiagnosed as a contributing factor.

Not every man experiences all of these symptoms. Some notice changes in libido first; others feel the mental and energy effects before any physical changes — which is why lab testing is essential for an accurate diagnosis.

What Causes Testosterone Levels to Drop?

Testosterone levels decline for a variety of reasons. Some are age-related; others reflect underlying health conditions or lifestyle factors. Common causes include:

  • Chronic stress
  • Poor or insufficient sleep
  • Obesity
  • Type 2 diabetes
  • Certain medications
  • Conditions affecting the pituitary gland or testes

Understanding the cause matters because it determines which type of treatment is most appropriate.

Primary vs. Secondary Hypogonadism

Primary hypogonadism occurs when the testes themselves are not producing enough testosterone. The brain is signaling correctly; the testes are failing to respond.

Secondary hypogonadism occurs when the hypothalamus or pituitary gland is not sending adequate signals to the testes. The testes may be capable of producing testosterone but aren't being prompted to do so.

This distinction directly affects treatment selection. TRT works for both types, but enclomiphene is specifically effective in secondary hypogonadism, where the signaling pathway can be restored.

How Is Low Testosterone Diagnosed?

Diagnosis requires two things: symptoms and bloodwork confirming low levels. Neither alone is sufficient for a responsible clinical diagnosis.

A comprehensive hormone panel typically includes:

  • Total testosterone — the primary diagnostic marker
  • Free testosterone — the biologically active fraction
  • SHBG — which affects how much testosterone is available to the body
  • LH and FSH — pituitary signals that help distinguish primary from secondary hypogonadism
  • Estradiol — elevated estrogen can contribute to symptoms
  • Prolactin — elevated levels can suppress testosterone and may indicate a pituitary issue

Testing is done in the morning when testosterone levels are at their peak. A single low result is often confirmed with a repeat test before treatment begins.

Treatment Options for Low Testosterone

Treatment selection depends on age, fertility goals, symptom severity, and lab results. The two most widely used, evidence-based options are testosterone replacement therapy (TRT) and enclomiphene.

Testosterone Replacement Therapy (TRT)

TRT involves administering external testosterone — most commonly through weekly injections. It's a direct and highly effective approach to raising testosterone levels.

TRT can:

  • Rapidly increase total and free testosterone
  • Improve libido and erectile function
  • Increase lean muscle mass
  • Lift mood and energy levels

For men who need consistent, predictable symptom relief, it is one of the most effective options available.

The key trade-off is that TRT suppresses the body's own hormone production. When external testosterone is introduced, the brain reduces its signals to the testes, which lowers LH and FSH levels and can significantly reduce sperm production. For men not concerned about fertility, this is generally acceptable. TRT requires ongoing treatment and regular lab monitoring to ensure levels stay in the optimal range.

Enclomiphene

Enclomiphene works through a fundamentally different mechanism. Rather than replacing testosterone, it stimulates the body's own production.

Enclomiphene is a selective estrogen receptor modulator (SERM). It blocks estrogen receptors at the hypothalamus, which causes the brain to increase its output of LH and FSH — the hormones that signal the testes to produce testosterone. This approach restores testosterone levels from within the body's own system, which means sperm production is preserved rather than suppressed.

Enclomiphene is often the preferred option for:

  • Younger men
  • Men who want to maintain fertility
  • Men diagnosed with secondary hypogonadism

Clinical research confirms it can restore testosterone to normal ranges while keeping sperm counts intact.

TRT vs. Enclomiphene: Which Is Right for You?

TRT

  • Mechanism: Direct testosterone replacement
  • Effect on fertility: Suppresses sperm production
  • Best for: Men not concerned about fertility; consistent, predictable results
  • Testosterone levels: Highly controllable and predictable
  • Monitoring required: Yes

Enclomiphene

  • Mechanism: Stimulates the body's own natural testosterone production
  • Effect on fertility: Preserves sperm production
  • Best for: Younger men; secondary hypogonadism; fertility preservation
  • Testosterone levels: Dependent on pituitary response
  • Monitoring required: Yes

There is no universally better option — only the one that aligns with your labs, symptoms, and goals. A qualified provider will evaluate all of these factors before recommending a protocol.

Can Lifestyle Changes Improve Testosterone?

For men with mildly low or borderline testosterone, lifestyle changes can produce meaningful improvements:

  • Consistent strength training
  • Weight loss in men who are overweight
  • 7–9 hours of sleep per night
  • Managing chronic stress
  • Limiting alcohol intake

All of these have been shown to support healthy testosterone levels. That said, lifestyle changes are generally not sufficient to correct clinically low testosterone on their own. If labs confirm a true deficiency, medical treatment is typically necessary to bring levels back to a healthy range.

When Should You Get Tested?

Consider getting your testosterone levels checked if you experience:

  • Persistent low sex drive
  • Ongoing or unexplained fatigue
  • Unexplained loss of muscle mass or strength
  • Erectile dysfunction
  • Reduced motivation and difficulty concentrating

The sooner low testosterone is identified, the sooner a personalized treatment plan can be developed.

Frequently Asked Questions

What testosterone level is considered low?

Most clinical guidelines define low testosterone as a total testosterone level below 300 ng/dL, combined with symptoms. Both components are necessary for a proper diagnosis. A man at 290 ng/dL with no symptoms may not require treatment, while a man at 310 ng/dL with significant symptoms may still warrant thorough evaluation.

Can low testosterone cause erectile dysfunction?

Yes. Testosterone is essential for libido and plays a significant supporting role in erectile function. Low levels can reduce sexual desire and contribute to difficulty achieving or maintaining erections. However, ED often has multiple contributing factors, so a full evaluation is important.

Is low testosterone common?

Yes, and it becomes increasingly prevalent with age. Testosterone naturally declines after age 30, and symptomatic low testosterone affects a meaningful percentage of adult men, with rates rising significantly in older age groups. Lifestyle and metabolic factors — including obesity and poor sleep — can accelerate the decline.

Is TRT safe?

When prescribed and monitored by a qualified physician, TRT is considered safe for most healthy men. Key monitoring parameters include red blood cell count, estradiol levels, and testosterone levels to ensure dosing remains appropriate. Men with certain conditions such as untreated sleep apnea or active prostate concerns may require additional evaluation before starting TRT.

Is enclomiphene better than TRT?

It depends on your situation. Enclomiphene is often preferred for men with secondary hypogonadism who want to preserve fertility, as it raises testosterone while maintaining sperm production. TRT provides more direct and predictable results but suppresses natural production. A physician can help determine which is better suited to your goals and labs.

How long does it take for low testosterone treatment to work?

Results vary by treatment type and individual. Many men notice improvements in energy and mood within a few weeks. Sexual symptoms, body composition, and muscle-related changes typically take several months to fully manifest. Lab values are usually re-checked at 6–12 weeks to assess response and adjust dosing if needed.
